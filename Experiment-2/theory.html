Introduction to Entity–Relationship (ER) Modeling

Entity–Relationship (ER) modeling is a conceptual database design technique used to model the structure and relationships of data in a database system. The ER model provides a graphical representation of entities, their attributes, and the relationships between entities. It serves as a bridge between real-world requirements and the implementation of a relational database.

Entities

An entity is a real-world object or concept that can be distinctly identified. Entities are represented by rectangles in ER diagrams. Examples include Student, Course, Employee, Department, etc. Each entity has a set of attributes that describe its properties. 

Examples:
- Student (student_id, name, email)
- Course (course_id, title, credits)
- Employee (emp_id, name, salary)

Attributes

Attributes are properties or characteristics of entities. They are represented by ovals or ellipses connected to their entity. Attributes can be simple (atomic), composite (made of sub-attributes), single-valued, multi-valued, or derived (calculated from other attributes). 

Types:
- Simple: age, salary
- Composite: address (street, city)
- Multi-valued: phone_numbers
- Derived: age (from DOB)

Relationships

A relationship represents an association between two or more entities. Relationships are represented by diamonds in ER diagrams. They can be one-to-one (1:1), one-to-many (1:N), or many-to-many (M:N). 

Examples:
- Student ENROLLS Course (M:N)
- Department HAS Employee (1:N)
- Person OWNS Passport (1:1)

Cardinality

Cardinality specifies the number of instances of one entity that can be associated with instances of another entity. It is represented by numbers (1, N, M) on relationship lines. Common cardinalities include 1:1, 1:N, and M:N. 

Cardinality Types:
- One-to-One (1:1)
- One-to-Many (1:N)
- Many-to-Many (M:N)

Participation Constraints

Participation constraints specify whether the existence of an entity depends on its relationship with another entity. Total participation (double line) means every entity must participate, while partial participation (single line) means some entities may not participate. 

Examples:
- Every employee MUST belong to a department (total)
- Not every student enrolls in a course (partial)

Weak Entities

A weak entity is an entity that cannot be uniquely identified by its attributes alone. It depends on a strong (owner) entity and is represented by a double rectangle. The relationship between weak and strong entities is called an identifying relationship. 

Example:
- Dependant (weak) depends on 
  Employee (strong entity)
- Primary key includes owner's key

ER Diagram Notation

Standard ER diagram notation uses specific symbols: rectangles for entities, ovals for attributes, diamonds for relationships, double rectangles for weak entities, and double lines for total participation. Primary keys are underlined, and relationship cardinalities are indicated by numbers on the relationship lines. 

Symbols:
- Rectangle: Entity
- Oval: Attribute
- Diamond: Relationship
- Double Rectangle: Weak Entity
- Underline: Primary Key
- Double Line: Total Participation

Mapping ER Diagrams to Relational Schema

The process of converting an ER diagram to a relational database schema involves several mapping rules:

  • Strong Entity: Create a relation (table) with all attributes. Primary key becomes the table's primary key.
  • Weak Entity: Create a relation including the primary key of the owner entity as a foreign key.
  • 1:1 Relationship: Include foreign key in either of the participating entities.
  • 1:N Relationship: Include foreign key in the entity on the "many" side.
  • M:N Relationship: Create a separate relation with foreign keys from both entities.
  • Composite Attributes: Flatten into separate columns in the relation.
  • Multi-valued Attributes: Create a separate relation or use a separate column.

Practical Example

Consider a university database with entities: Student, Course, and Department

ER Diagram:
- Student (student_id, name, email)
- Course (course_id, title, credits)
- Department (dept_id, dept_name)
- Student ENROLLS Course (M:N)
- Department OFFERS Course (1:N)

Relational Schema:
Student(student_id, name, email, dept_id)
Course(course_id, title, credits, dept_id)
Department(dept_id, dept_name)
Enrollment(student_id, course_id, grade)
